Arturo Valenzuela is Professor of Government and Director of the Center for
Latin American Studies in the Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service. Prior
to joining the Georgetown faculty in 1987 he was Professor of Political
Science and Director of the Council of Latin American Studies at Duke
University. He has been a Visiting Scholar at Oxford University, the
University of Sussex, the University of Florence, the University of Chile and
the Catholic University of Chile and a Fellow at the Woodrow Wilson
International Center for Scholars. He earned a B.A. summa cum laude from Drew
University and the M.A. and Ph.D. degrees in Political Science from Columbia
University. He is fluent in French and Spanish.

During President Bill Clinton's second term in office, Dr. Valenzuela served
at the White House as Special Assistant to the President and Senior Director
for Inter-American Affairs on the National Security Council. In that role he
advised the President and the National Security Adviser on foreign, defense,
intelligence, economic and other policy issues concerning the Western
Hemisphere, managed the formulation and implementation of multilateral and
bilateral foreign policy initiatives in the Americas, and directed U.S.
responses to regional crises.

Dr. Valenzuela served as Deputy Assistant Secretary for Inter-American Affairs
in the United States Department of State in the first Clinton Administration.
A White House political appointee, he was named to the Senior Executive
Service of the United States of America. His responsibilities included global
issues (democracy, environment, human rights, migration and refugees) for the
Americas and the formulation and implementation of U.S. foreign policy toward
Mexico. For his academic and foreign policy contributions, the government of
Brazil honored him with the National Order of the Southern Cross.

A specialist on the origins and consolidation of democracy, Latin American
politics, electoral systems, civil-military relations, political parties,
regime transitions and U.S.-Latin American relations, Dr. Valenzuela is an
expert on Chilean politics, the politics of the Southern Cone and Mexican
politics. He is the author or co-author of nine books including The Breakdown
of Democratic Regimes: Chile and A Nation of Enemies: Chile Under Pinochet.
His recent work has focused on the consolidation of democracy. With Juan J.
Linz he published The Failure of Presidential Democracy and with Scott
Mainwaring, Politics, Society and Democracy: Latin America. He is the author
of several dozen scholarly articles that have appeared in edited collections
and scholarly journals including Comparative Politics, Foreign Policy, Foreign
Affairs, Rivista Italiana di Scienza Politica, Estudios P¨²blicos and the Latin
American Research Review. He has been a member of the editorial boards of the
Journal of Democracy, Current History, Am¨¦rique Latine, and The Third World
Quarterly and has published op-eds in leading newspapers in the United States,
Latin America and Europe.

His board memberships have included the National Democratic Institute for
International Affairs (NDI), the Hispanic Council for International Relations
(HCIR), and America's Watch. He is a member of the Council on Foreign
Relations and several academic societies. He has also been a consultant to the
Senate Democratic Policy Committee, international affairs advisor for the
National Council of La Raza, and an advisor on political and constitutional
reform issues in Bolivia, Chile, Brazil, Ecuador and Colombia. He has been a
consultant to the Ford Foundation, the Inter-American Foundation, and various
private sector firms and organizations.

Dr. Valenzuela's media appearances include ABC's Nightline, NBC's Today Show,
The News Hour with Jim Lehrer, CNN (Cross Fire, Q&A;, International), the CBS
Evening News, Univisi¨®n, National Public Radio (Talk of the Nation, Morning
Edition, Weekend, Edition, All Things Considered, Fresh Air), C-Span, BBC
World Service, and other media outlets in the United States, Latin America and
Europe. Until he joined the administration he was a regular host of CNN
Spanish's Choque de Opiniones, a program modeled on CNN's Crossfire.

**Spring 2002:  
**_GOVT 548 U.S. Foreign Policy-making in Latin America_ (Syllabus)  
This course is an overview of U.S. Latin American relations during the Clinton
Administration. It describes the formulation of U.S. foreign policy toward the
Hemisphere, assess the overall state of bilateral relations with major
countries, and evaluate the growing trend toward regional multilateralism. The
instructor served as Deputy Assistant Secretary of State in the first term of
the Clinton administration and Special Assistant to the President and Senior
Director for Inter-American Affairs in the National Security Council, in the
second term. **
